General Research Interests

Adaptation, gene flow, evolutionary dynamics, genome organization, phylogeography, disturbance

In particular, I am interested in the evolution of hierarchically structured populations exhibiting extensive gene flow and the inferences available regarding evolutionary and demographic processes from genetic data under both equilibrium and non-equilibrium scenarios. How do evolutionary processes influence the genetic and genomic architectures of complex traits? How does the genetic and genomic architecture influence resilience to change? How does adaptation influence, and how is it influenced by, recombination rate evolution - are their feedbacks? How do these patterns and processes differ across closely related species/genera? What are the implications of these findings to future analyses to uncover the loci underlying adaptive traits, as well as to conservation, industry, the economy, and our overall understanding of evolution across taxa?

Dissertation background (under construction)

Effects of Forest Management on Fine-scale Gene Flow in a Fire-suppressed population of Pinus lambertiana Dougl.

Effect of fire supression on forest crowding in the upper Yosemite Valley from Columbia Point (source unknown)

Frequent, low-severity fires in historic western North American forests were once a major driver of ecological patterns and processes, creating resilient systems dominated by widely spaced pine species. However, a century of fire-suppression has caused overcrowding, altering forest composition to shade-tolerant species while increasing competition, leaving trees stressed and susceptible to pathogens, insects, and high-severity fire. Exacerbating the issue, the probability of fire incidence is expected to increase across western forests with changing climate, while fire season has been observed to begin earlier and last longer than historic trends. As a result, forest thinning in western forests has been identified as the primary management tool to mitigate these risks. Yet little is known of how thinning, fire, or their inevitable interaction affect evolutionary processes of constituent, historically dominant pine species, specifically regarding processes which influence fitness and play an important role in the opportunity for selection and population persistence. To ensure sustainable timber harvest and wildlife habitat it is essential that forest managers understand the inevitable interplay between anthropogenic and natural disturbance on these underlying evolutionary processes.

We addressed relevant and understudied issues regarding the evolutionary impact of widely practiced forest management in California. Previous studies of North American forest management across systems often used indices of heterozygosity (e.g., FST, Ho, He, alleleic richness, etc) that are underinformative to assess management impact, particularly when the treated stands are often geographically adjacent, and populations are subsampled. Such studies lack detailed examination of evolutionary processes affected by forest treatment, specifically those influencing fitness, making extrapolation of results difficult for even the current, let alone other, systems.

The goal of this project were to understand the effects of fire, thinning, and their interaction on evolutionary processes influencing fitness within a widespread, fire-suppressed, historically dominant pine species. Specifically, we asked two main questions: 1) How do fire and thinning management affect contemporary gene flow within sugar pine (Pinus lambertiana)? 2) How do fire and thinning management affect the mating system of this ecologically important species (e.g., the proportion of outcrossing, biparental inbreeding, correlation of outcross paternity, and the number of effective pollen donors)? We will address these questions through results of parentage analysis (sensu Moran & Clark 2011) using plastid and nuclear markers in sugar pine across factorial treatment plots at Teakettle Experimental Forest, an established long-term USFS study site set against a backdrop of fire-suppression (see 3D stem map to the left).
